Synopsis

A coordinated online framework combining Building Information Modelling (BIM), the Internet of Things (IoT), Artificial Intelligence (AI), and digital twins is replacing fragmented, manual processes in the construction industry as part of a digital transformation that aims to improve collaboration and lifecycle productivity. The hypothetical G+3 residential block with ground-floor parking, three repeating residential floors and a central staircase core situated in a restricted urban setting is the subject of this Smart Construction Implementation Plan. Through the integration of these cutting-edge technologies, the project hopes to improve long-term sustainability results, lower mistake rates, and improve site safety by enabling real-time monitoring and predictive analysis. This particular four-story project was chosen because it offers the perfect setting for testing automation, data unification, and space optimisation techniques due to its structural intricacy and repeating components. In order to handle important lifecycle concerns including energy consumption, maintenance scheduling, and occupant safety, the implementation plan creates a comprehensive digital approach. The report assesses the use of BIM to coordinate architectural and structural elements, talks about the implementation of integrated digital technologies, and ends with a thorough plan and cost-benefit analysis to optimise operational performance by shifting to data-driven workflows.

For structural stability, the chosen G+3 residential building is constructed with reinforced concrete and uses a regular grid layout of slabs, columns, beams and stairs. The rectangular architectural massing improves constructability and functional efficiency with its projecting balconies and repeating pattern of window modules and railings. The three top residential floors are made up of repeating apartments arranged around a staircase core for emergency escape and vertical circulation, while the bottom floor is used for parking and the main entry lobby. In order to keep a realistic building sequence and keep living areas and vehicle traffic apart, this standardised plan is complemented with balconies for natural light and air circulation. Building Information Modelling (BIM) was used with Autodesk Revit to produce a comprehensive three-dimensional model that included all structural and architectural components in order to support this design. This coordinated model, which incorporates site elements, walls, slabs, columns, and beams, produces consistent elevations, sections, floor plans, and 3D views at a high Level of Development that are appropriate for quantitative extraction and visualisation. By combining data for lifetime management, maintenance planning, and cost control through accurate quantity take-offs, BIM functions as a crucial multidisciplinary tool that facilitates collision detection and spatial validation.

Building Information Modelling (BIM)

A comprehensive BIM model was developed to coordinate architectural and structural components.The main digital tool for controlling the residential building's design, construction, and operation is Building Information Modelling (BIM). It starts with a parametric three-dimensional model that combines site, structural, and architectural components. Interdisciplinary collaboration and proactive collision identification are made possible by the BIM process, which defines elements like as slabs, beams, and columns with integrated material and performance data. With the use of sophisticated visualisation tools, this approach enables project teams to spot and address spatial issues early in the design process, greatly cutting down on rework, material waste, and on-site delays while improving communication between designers, contractors, and customers.

The model's usefulness is further increased by integrating 4D scheduling and 5D cost estimates, which connects tangible elements to budgetary constraints and building schedules. This method offers automated, precise material estimates for better budget forecasting and procurement management, while also facilitating logical sequence planning for structural frame and façade construction. Structured data management and version control enable these procedures, which address typical implementation issues including software interoperability and staff training through standardised modelling principles and organised stakeholder education to guarantee project success.

Internet of Things (IoT)

IoT technologies are proposed for structural health monitoring and environmental sensing. Throughout the project lifespan, real-time monitoring, safety management, and operational efficiency are made possible by the G+3 residential building's incorporation of Internet of Things (IoT) technology. Important uses include environmental sensors to monitor temperature, humidity, and air quality for the best cure and interior comfort, and structural health monitoring using strain gauges and vibration sensors to spot irregularities. In order to optimise energy flows and lifecycle management, energy consumption and post-occupancy facility performance are measured at the unit level, while worker safety and material storage are also monitored throughout construction.

A real-time monitoring approach uses wireless connectivity to send sensor data to a centralised, cloud-based platform that is connected with the BIM environment in order to optimise the value of this data. With the help of this configuration, automatic logging and visualisation dashboards that display data using trend analyses and heat maps are made possible, enhancing situational awareness and enabling automated warnings in the event that predefined structural or safety limitations are crossed. Encrypted communication, secure access methods, and regular system audits to guarantee long-term data integrity mitigate possible dangers like cybersecurity and data privacy, even while the advantages include improved safety and lower maintenance costs.
